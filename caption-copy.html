Overview ​


​As accessibility becomes a greater priority for our users and clients, it is important to create solid, inclusive best practices and processes around the content that we produce and/or include in our digital offerings. Multimedia is no exception. To ensure time-based media content is accessible to the greatest number of people possible:
  • ​​For Audio-only content, such as recorded podcasts, interviews, etc., provide a transcript.
  • For Video-only content, such as animations provide a transcript and an audio description, which describes the contents of the video.
  • For Video and Audio content, provide captions and a transcript. If information is communicated visually yet not through dialogue, provide audio description.


Captions


Captions are the textual representation of video content. They include:

  • spoken dialogue
  • speaker identification (unlike subtitles)
  • information about non-dialogue sounds, such as laughter, applause, etc.


Audio Description

Audio descriptions enable those who are blind or visually impaired to receive the same information provided visually in media (see 1.2.5 Audio Description (Prerecorded) Level AA (Opens in new window)). Audio descriptions are meant to provide information on visual content that is considered essential to the comprehension of the content. Audio descriptions provide information about significant visual details that cannot be understood from the main soundtrack alone.


Transcripts


Transcripts provide a textual representation of audio and video content. Providing a transcript is particularly important in cases where a user is deaf/blind and uses a device, such as a braille reader, to access content on the web. They should include:

  • spoken dialogue
  • information about significant non-dialogue sounds, such as laughter, applause, etc.
  • visual details, such as “showing a mock-up of your website”


Multimedia FAQ

​

Except for Advanced Job Description (AJD) videos, which always require audio descriptions and transcripts and audio-only files which always require transcripts, Radancy is taking a phased approach to creating more accessible multimedia. For multimedia that sits outside of the AJD page type, captions for video and transcripts for audio-only content, must always be provided, while audio descriptions and transcripts for video will be required on an as needed basis. However, there is nothing stopping a team from providing optimally accessible multimedia today, so if you wish to include audio descriptions and transcripts for video, or if a client requires them, please consult with Michael “Spell” Spellacy for more details. Below you will find a list of frequently asked questions regarding the creation of accessible multimedia:

My video is hosted on YouTube. Don’t they create captions automatically? Am I still responsible?

Yes, you are responsible. If your video is hosted on YouTube or another third-party service that creates automatic captions, it is very important to review the generated captions for accuracy against any spoken dialog in the video. If the captions prove to be inaccurate as to impact the understanding of the video, then steps should be taken to resolve the issue by first discussing the problem with your client. If they agree to produce new captions, Radancy can perform this task on their behalf via a third-party vendor, but it will be up to the client to implement the deliverable we provide, through the YouTube administration tool. See Add your own subtitles and closed captions (Opens in new window).

What about third-party hosting that does not provide automatic captions?

You are responsible for this, too. Again, this is something that should be discussed with your clients. While we can produce captions for our clients, it is they who will again be responsible for implementing them. Popular alternatives to YouTube, such as Vimeo, also have administrative features to include captions. See Vimeo: Captions and subtitles (Opens in new window).

We are hosting an audio file or podcast for a client. How do we make these more accessible?

Captions are not required in this case, but a text-based transcription will be required and will need to be worked into the design, usually in the form of a disclosure that can toggle the visability of the transcript (Modals are not reccomended). Radancy can also produce transcripts, via a third-party vendor.

We are self-hosting a video with spoken dialog. Am I responsible for captions?

If the client provides us with a video to self-host, then we should ask if captions are also an available asset. If they are not available, then yes, Radancy will need to produce captions for our development team to implement. This can be done through a third-party vendor. More details for acquiring captions may be found in the process section of this document. Currently, providing captions is the minimal effort Radancy needs to perform to produce more accessible video outside of AJD, but we intend to improve the quality of our multimedia implementations at a later date, to include audio descriptions and transcripts.

My video has no spoken dialog, do I still need a caption?

Until further notice, only AJD videos, which typically have no spoken dialog, but contain important information, require a verbatim audio description (a WebVTT file with time stamped text that syncs with content appearing in video) and a text-based fallback that can be leveraged as a transcript. Our creative centers have been trained and are responsible for providing these two assets to development. In the future, new features will be implemented on AJD and more training provided to make this experience more robust for people with disabilities. If clients or development deem that more equivalents are required for non-speech videos outside of AJD, then this will be handled on a case-by- case basis. Our current caption vendor does not produce audio descriptions, but services that do, are being investigated.

What is the process for acquiring captions or transcripts for self-hosted multimedia?

All multimedia created by Radancy must ensure, at a minimum, captions for video with spoken dialogue and transcripts for audio-only content. If a client has provided the multimedia, then they should be asked to provide required assets. If the client does not have the needed assets, we can let them know that Radancy can produce them on their behalf for an extra cost.  The following steps should then be taken to produce what we require:

  1. DPMs will submit requests to Creative Production Manager along with the video or audio that requires needed assets
  2. Production Manager will open a job and create an estimate for the captions or transcripts for the multimedia
  3. DPM to send estimate to Account Team and wait for client approval
  4. Once signed estimate or approval is provided to the Production Manager the assets needed will be created internally or through a third party vendor
  5. Completed assets will be sent the DPM for the development team to implement. Note: Only WebVTT files (*.vtt) will be accepted by our development team for self-hosted video.

What is a WebVT​T file?

A WebVTT (Web Video Text Track) file, or VTT for short due to its file extension (*.vtt), is a simple, text-based file that can be included in HTML5 video to produce captions and audio descriptions. Each line of text within the file is time stamped so that it may be presented in sync with spoken dialog or other cues, during playback of the video. You can see an example on CodePen (Opens in new window).

WebVTT Formatting

Also note that if you are manually creating your own WebVTT file, then it must be properly formatted. It is very important that the first line contain the term "WEBVTT". 

You can learn more about WebVTT on MDN Web Docs (Opens in new window).
